# BestPhilippines Brand Voice Guide

*How we sound. What we say. What we don't.*

**Version 1.0 · April 2026**

---

## Who this guide is for

Anyone writing for BestPhilippines — Martin, Yahnee, future critics, future copywriters, future social media hires, future newsletter editors, and any AI tools used in content generation. If your name goes on something that ships under the BestPhilippines masthead, read this first.

A voice guide exists to keep the publication's tone consistent when the author isn't. By month 12 you won't be writing every verdict yourself. This document is how the voice survives growth.

---

## The voice in one paragraph

BestPhilippines sounds like the friend at dinner who has actually thought about food, eats out more than is healthy, takes the subject seriously but refuses to take *itself* seriously. We're confident without being precious. We're specific without being smug. We're Filipino without being folklore. We score sharply but we never punch down. We're here to help you pick dinner, not to perform expertise.

If our voice had a patron saint, it would be Anthony Bourdain, not Pete Wells. Warm before sharp. Affection before authority. Curious before certain.

## The five voice principles

### 1. Specificity over superlatives

**Not:** *Amazing food in a beautiful space.*
**Yes:** *The sisig arrives on a cast-iron plate still hissing, and the lime wedge on the side is doing real work.*

Every adjective earns its place or gets cut. "Amazing," "incredible," "must-try," "hidden gem," "elevated," "transcendent," "unique," "authentic," "memorable" — these are the words that make every food blog read like every other food blog. We don't use them. If we did our job, you can tell what the place is like without us reaching for an intensifier.

### 2. Opinions, held clearly

**Not:** *Some might say the service could be improved.*
**Yes:** *Service was slow. Twice.*

We have opinions. We state them. We don't hedge, soften, or attribute them to imaginary other people. If we think the cocktails are overpriced, we write that. If we think the chef's previous restaurant was better, we write that. "Some diners may find" is cowardice. "We think" is honesty. "It is" is confidence. All three are more useful than evasion.

### 3. Warmth as the baseline, sharpness as the tool

The default posture is warm. Filipino food culture is generous — we reflect that. We root for good restaurants. We celebrate when a place earns a high score. We take obvious pleasure in a meal done well.

Sharpness comes out when earned. When a celebrity chef is coasting. When a hyped opening is a letdown. When a beloved institution has slipped and everyone is pretending otherwise. Sharpness is a tool we pick up deliberately, not a mood we live in.

Never sharp at small targets. A neighborhood karinderya does not get a 4.2 takedown. A celebrity chef's hyped opening might. The rule: the more public the profile, the more criticism is fair. Punching down is not the voice.

### 4. Filipino without cosplay

We write from the Philippines for a primarily Filipino audience, with visitors welcome. This means:

- Filipino dish names are not italicized. Sinigang is sinigang, not *sinigang.* We don't other our own food.
- Filipino slang appears when natural. Titos, titas, barkada, merienda — these are used without quotation marks or translation. If a foreign reader needs to google it once, that's fine.
- We don't explain obvious context. We don't write "Poblacion, Manila's nightlife district." We write "Poblacion." Readers who don't know will learn by reading.
- We avoid the tourist register. No "exotic." No "hidden in the bustling streets of." No "a symphony of flavors." Anything a Condé Nast travel essay would write, we don't.
- Regional specificity matters. Bicol is not Pampanga is not Cebu is not Iloilo. A sinigang from one region is not interchangeable with another. Writers who can't tell them apart don't write about them.

### 5. Useful before beautiful

A verdict that helps someone decide where to eat tonight is worth more than a sentence that sings. If we can get both, we do. If we have to choose, we choose useful.

**Not:** *A marriage of tradition and innovation.*
**Yes:** *The kare-kare is traditional. The paella negra is not. The paella is the better dish.*

## Sentence-level craft

### Length and rhythm

Mix long and short. A paragraph of identical-length sentences is a paragraph that puts readers to sleep. Read drafts aloud. If you run out of breath, cut.

**Good rhythm:** *We went on a Tuesday. The room was half-empty. The sisig was perfect.*

**Bad rhythm:** *We went to the restaurant on a Tuesday evening at around eight o'clock, and we found that the dining room was approximately half-empty at that time, and the sisig dish that we ordered turned out to be perfect in our opinion.*

### First-person plural, not first-person singular

We use *we.* Not *I.* Even when one editor writes it, the voice is the publication's. This matters for consistency and for keeping individual ego out of the editorial.

Exception: Phase 2 named-critic long-form reviews use "I." The distinction is deliberate — algorithmic entries and editor's picks are the publication's voice; critic reviews are a specific person's.

### Verbs do the work

Strong verbs beat strong adjectives. A restaurant doesn't have "incredible energy" — it hums, it clatters, it roars, it murmurs. The food doesn't offer "bold flavors" — it hits, it builds, it lingers, it disappears.

### Concrete over abstract

Don't say "the service was attentive." Say "our glasses were topped up three times without being asked." One is a claim, the other is evidence.

### Cut qualifiers ruthlessly

Words to watch for and usually delete: *very, quite, rather, fairly, somewhat, pretty (as intensifier), really, truly, literally, actually, basically, essentially, simply, just.* These words almost always weaken the sentence they're in. Cut them first, restore only if the sentence is worse without.

## Words we don't use

These are the tells that mark generic food writing. We ban them from BestPhilippines content.

### Banned superlatives
- Amazing, incredible, awesome, phenomenal, spectacular
- Must-try, must-visit, must-eat, must-order
- Transcendent, sublime, divine, heavenly
- Unforgettable, memorable, unique (almost nothing is actually unique)
- Game-changing, next-level, elevated, upscale

### Banned tourist-register
- Hidden gem, hidden treasure, best-kept secret
- Authentic (almost always meaningless when used by non-locals)
- Exotic (never)
- A symphony of [anything]
- A marriage of [anything]
- Tucked away, nestled in
- Vibrant, bustling (for places), charming (for anything)
- Foodie (we don't call ourselves this; we don't call others this)

### Banned hedges
- Arguably (we argue or we don't)
- Some might say, some may find
- It could be argued
- Perhaps, possibly, maybe (usually)

### Banned constructions
- "In my opinion" (if we're saying it, obviously)
- "I feel like" (we don't feel, we judge)
- "At the end of the day" (an exhausted phrase)
- "When all is said and done" (same)
- "Needless to say" (then don't say it)
- "It is what it is" (never)

### Banned Filipino-food-media clichés
- "A love letter to [Filipino cuisine]"
- "Elevated Filipino food" (our food does not require elevation)
- "A taste of home"
- "Flavors that transport you"
- "Lolas would approve" (unless you're actually quoting a real lola)

## How to write a one-line verdict

The one-line verdict is the editorial voice surfaced on every venue entry. It appears below the venue name on cards, lists, and entry pages. You will write hundreds of these. They are the most-read writing on the site.

### The formula

One sentence. Maximum 20 words. Specific, opinionated, earned.

The verdict should answer one question: *what is the single most useful thing to know about this place before you decide to go?*

### Good verdicts

- *The only ramyun in Poblacion that earns the 1 AM walk.*
- *Sisig that has quietly been the best in Makati for a decade, no matter what the new openings claim.*
- *Worth it for the bone marrow. Skip the pasta.*
- *The wine list is the reason. The food is the excuse.*
- *Reliable at 7 PM on a Tuesday. Chaos at 9 PM on a Saturday. Plan accordingly.*
- *Lechon that justifies the drive to Alabang. Not lechon that justifies the wait.*
- *Cebuano kitchen in Makati that doesn't apologize for being Cebuano.*

### Bad verdicts

- *A great spot for Korean food.* (tells you nothing specific)
- *Must-try for anyone visiting Manila.* (banned words; no information)
- *A hidden gem with incredible flavors.* (banned words; no information)
- *The perfect restaurant for any occasion.* (no restaurant is this)
- *Highly recommended!* (exclamation mark; no content)

### Structural patterns that work

- **The concession:** *Expensive. Worth it.*
- **The contrast:** *Ambitious menu, ordinary execution.*
- **The scene-setter:** *For the 11 PM barkada reunion, not the 7 PM first date.*
- **The naming:** *The best [specific dish] in [specific place].*
- **The warning:** *Book three weeks out, or don't bother.*
- **The defense:** *Been here forever, still better than most of what opened this year.*

### Rules

- One sentence unless the content genuinely requires two
- No exclamation marks (ever, unless quoting someone)
- No banned words
- No qualifiers (no "arguably," "perhaps," "maybe")
- Specific enough that a reader who knows the place recognizes it
- Opinionated enough that someone could disagree with it

## How to write a newsletter lede

The Monday Briefing opens with two to three sentences before the three picks. This is the editorial voice at its most concentrated.

### The formula

Observation → stance → promise.

**Observation:** something specific about the week, the season, the scene.
**Stance:** an opinion about it, however small.
**Promise:** what the reader is about to get.

### Example

*Holy Week is over. Restaurants that took the week off are back, some of them rustier than they'd like to admit. We ate at three places last week that are worth the post-holiday rebound.*

### What to avoid

- Opening with "Hello" or "Hi there" (too soft)
- Opening with "This week" (too flat)
- Opening with a banned superlative
- Opening with a question (usually lazy)
- Opening with weather unless the weather is genuinely the point

## Tone by context

The voice stays the same; the register shifts slightly based on where the writing lives.

| Context | Register | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Venue entry verdict | Sharpest, shortest | One sentence, no filler |
| Canon list blurbs | Confident, warmer | 2–3 sentences, celebrates the inclusion |
| Monday Briefing | Conversational, lightly personal | "We" voice, week-specific |
| About / Scoring / Policies pages | Plainspoken, serious, occasionally playful | Credibility documents — warmth but not jokes in critical sections |
| Social posts | Shorter, punchier | No banned words apply more strictly here |
| Email replies (support, press) | Warm, professional | "Thanks for writing" is fine; don't perform the voice at people |
| Phase 2 critic long-form | Most expansive, most writerly | Voice guide still applies; length opens up |

## Handling sensitive topics

### Closures, failures, losses

When a venue we've scored closes, we cover it soberly. A good restaurant closing is a real loss to the scene. We don't pile on. We don't use closures to gloat about low scores. We mark the closing, note what the place meant, move on.

### Takedowns (Phase 2)

Sub-5.0 named-critic reviews are the rarest and highest-stakes pieces we publish. Rules:
- Never about a small venue. Only targets with significant public profile.
- Fact-checked by a second editor before publication.
- Reviewed by libel counsel.
- Written with precision, not performance. The voice gets *more* controlled in a takedown, not less. Anger makes bad writing.
- The goal is useful criticism, not spectacle.

### Owner or chef troubles

Personal legal issues, allegations, arrests, controversies: we do not cover them unless they directly affect the dining experience (kitchen closure, staff turnover, ownership change). We are a dining publication, not a gossip column.

### Factual errors by venues

When a restaurant misrepresents itself — claims a star chef who isn't there, markets ingredients it doesn't use, inflates awards — we can note this in a verdict. Sparingly. Specifically. With sourcing.

## Filipino context and cultural register

### Language

Primary language: English, Philippine register. Not American, not British. Our spelling is American (color, not colour) but our phrasing is Filipino-English (we "go down" from a car, we "make kulit," we "take home" rather than "take out").

Taglish is welcome in headlines, social, and occasional verdicts where it serves the specific content. Not used for its own sake.

### Dish names

- Filipino dish names: unitalicized, untranslated (sinigang, kare-kare, sisig, kinilaw, adobo, lechon, kilawin)
- Japanese dish names: unitalicized after first appearance if context is clear
- French dish names: unitalicized if in common use (confit, tartare), italicized if rare (*île flottante*)
- Translation only when genuinely needed for a non-Filipino reader to follow — and only the first time

### Regional awareness

Writers must know basic regional distinctions. A partial list:
- Pampanga / Kapampangan — sisig originated here, bringhe, tocino traditions
- Bicol — coconut milk, chili forward, Bicol Express, laing
- Ilocos — bagnet, empanada, saltiness, vinegar
- Cebu / Visayas — lechon (the serious lechon), puso, kinilaw traditions
- Iloilo — batchoy, la paz
- Mindanao — piyaya traditions, halal, regional complexity we cover with care

Writing about a region's food without knowing the region is how food media loses credibility. When in doubt, ask.

### Names and honorifics

- Chefs and owners referred to by full name on first mention, last name after (*Chef Jordy Navarra* → *Navarra*)
- Do not use "Chef" before every mention; it gets tedious
- Respect Filipino honorifics when they're genuinely in use (Manang, Kuya, Ate when we're describing how a place refers to its own staff)

## How to push back on the voice guide

This document is version 1.0. It will be wrong about things. When you find a place where the voice guide is steering you toward worse writing, flag it. Voice guides exist to support good writing, not to constrain it.

Process: edit the Google Doc, leave a comment, tag Martin and Yahnee. We update this document quarterly and publish the changelog internally.

---

## The one test

Before publishing anything under the BestPhilippines masthead, ask:

**Would a Filipino food writer I respect nod at this, or roll their eyes?**

If you can't confidently answer "nod," revise.
